#### Background

- **Role**: VP of Engineering at a 200-person SaaS company
- **Experience**: 15 years in tech leadership; manages 6 engineering teams across 3 product lines
- **Tech comfort**: Low for daily tool use; delegates task-level work but needs high-level visibility on demand

#### Goals

- Get a single-screen portfolio view of all active programs, their health, and budget status
- Identify at-risk projects and resource conflicts without digging into task details
- Make strategic prioritization decisions based on real data, not status meeting anecdotes
- Share progress with the board and investors with minimal preparation

#### Pain Points

- PM tools are built for practitioners — too much detail, too many clicks to get a summary
- Has to ask project managers to compile status updates manually every week
- No easy way to see cross-team dependencies or competing resource demands at the portfolio level
- Dashboards that require configuration before they're useful — she needs value on first login

#### Usage Scenarios

1. **Weekly leadership review**: Opens a portfolio dashboard showing health status (green/yellow/red) for each program, budget burn rate, and milestone completion percentage. Drills into a yellow project to see the top 3 blockers — nothing deeper.
2. **Board preparation**: Exports a one-page summary showing quarterly delivery against OKRs, headcount utilization, and a timeline of upcoming milestones across all product lines.
3. **Resource arbitration**: Two teams need the same specialist. Views a resource allocation chart comparing workload and upcoming commitments to make a data-driven decision.
4. **Ad-hoc check-in**: Gets a Slack notification that a project turned red. Opens TaskForge on her phone, sees the summary — a critical dependency is blocked — and adds a comment tagging the responsible lead.
